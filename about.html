About

Research in simulation, world models, and environment dynamics.

Applied Worlds publishes original, empirical work on how environments evolve, how to simulate them faithfully, and how agents learn and decide inside complex systems.

The systems I care about — financial markets, multi-agent environments, physical systems under adaptive control — share a structure: they are non-stationary, partially observable, and endogenously shaped by the agents acting inside them. Modelling them well enough to learn from, plan with, and decide inside is the common problem across all three research threads here.

I approach these questions empirically. A hypothesis gets written, an experiment gets designed, an implementation gets built, and results get published — including the failures. The goal is a public, honest record of what works and what does not, not a curated highlight reel of successes.

Applied Worlds is the Systems leg of a three-site research ecosystem. It connects to Applied Markets — how agents behave in financial environments — and Applied Models — how neural representations form internally. The three questions are complementary: Systems × Behavior × Intelligence.

Research Threads Three
01
Thread I

World Models

Rooted in the Ha & Schmidhuber line of work, extended. Learning a latent representation of how a system evolves — then dreaming in that space to plan without touching the real environment. The control engineering foundation (state-space representations, stability analysis, dynamical systems) connects here naturally, reframed through modern deep learning and physics-informed neural network architectures developed during CRIS Lab research.

02
Thread II

Agent-Environment Dynamics

The feedback loop question. When an agent acts, the environment changes, which changes the agent's next observation, which shapes the agent's next action. In markets this is reflexivity. In multi-agent RL this is non-stationarity — every agent's policy is part of every other agent's environment. In macroeconomics this is the Lucas critique. The convergence of RL work with Agrawal, causal inference training with Bareinboim, and behavioral economics roots from Mu Sigma makes this the densest thread.

Reflexivity Multi-Agent RL Non-Stationarity Lucas Critique Emergence
03
Thread III

Counterfactual Simulation

Simulation is only useful if you can intervene inside it, not just run it forward. Structural causal models provide the formal language for "what if?" — and when encoded as simulable environments, they let you measure whether counterfactual reasoning actually improves decisions compared to purely observational baselines. Connects directly to the Rewire product architecture and to the Bareinboim causal inference curriculum.

Editorial Boundary Rules
No Learning Notes Not passive study output. Course notes, reading reflections, and literature summaries do not belong here.
No Reposting No paper summaries, no blog recaps. Every artifact is original work built on direct implementation.
No Tutorials Applied Worlds is not a teaching resource. Every artifact starts with a hypothesis, not an explanation.
Failures Published If the experiment does not work, that is published too. The record is the product.
Keep Moving Progress over optics. There is no content calendar. Artifacts ship when the experiment is done.
